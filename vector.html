Vector Analysis: Hospital accessibility and volunteer geographical information

I mapped accessibility of hospitals in Bratislava, the capital of my home country, and calculated the number of household in 5 and 10 minute driving distance from each hospital. As the second part of the project, together with other students we collected real-life data from the city of Utrecht and used them to map percieved safety and haughtedness.

Project Overview

Healthcare accesibility is a major concern in all human settlements. This project uses vector analysis to create a visual map showing area from which the nearest hospital can be reached within 5 or 10 minutes. Five minute accesibility is important for life threathening states such as stroke, when first aid needs to be provided within 6 minutes, otherwise permanent neural damage occurs, as neurons in our brain can survive up to 6 minutes without access to oxygen. Ten minute accesibility is a great advantage for all inhabitants, as anyone can experience an emergency situation with need of urgent care. I selected Bretislava, the capital od Slovakia, as the city has unusual shape with multiple natural barriers and I was interested what is the accesibility of hospitals in it.

Second part of this project is focused on real life volunteer data collection and percieved public safety. Volunteer data collection has seen wider and wider use in many scientific fields, such as ornithology, botany, metheorology, or carthogrpahy. Having practical experience collecting the data and later analyzing them gave me an important perspective on how such volunteers should be trained, how important is to iform volunteers about the purpose of the data they collect, and how subjective the data collection can be. We collected data during one afternoon in central and eastern areas of Utrecht and neighbouring towns. Due to not knowing purpose of the data, we chose suboptimal way of collection - we split into individuals and small groups to cover as much area as possible, which later impeded the intended analysis by gender of the collector. For the analysis, I used kernel density, which is a spatial analysis technique that turns point data into continuous surface. The resulting map is called heatmap and the spots with high kernel density areas called heatspots - such areas represents potential sources or risk areas for certain phenomenon.

Methods

The hospital accesibility mapping was done using isochrones tool, which is a QGIS tool that uses external computing server to create isochrones - shaded areas which borders are situated certain travel time from selected place. I downloaded and filtered data of buildings in Bratislava and created point layer with only hospitals. By using isochrones tool and OpenRouteService.org, I created the isochrones of 5 and 10 minute driving distance from the nearest hospital. By overlaying the building and isochrones layers I was able to calculate how many apartments are within each isochrone.

The data collection in Utrecht was done using ArcGIS Online survey. The data from the survey were transformed into vector point layer in ArcGIS Online and I analyzed them in QGIS. I used vector analysis tool to create a kernel density map - a heatmap. I selected the parameters of the kernel density function, namely radius, to 200 m, as the point data represent very specific locations and bigger radius would spread the "heat" from one point over a large area, distoriting the final result.

Results & Reflection

As we can see on the map of hospital accessibility above, most of the apartments within Bratislava are in 10 minute driving range from the nearest hospital. The data about number of apartments within range of a specific hospital can be used to optimize allocation of patients or selection of locations of new hospitals. However, it is important to realize that the isochrones are based purely on the distance and the road network and they do not take into consideration actual traffic. As someone who visited Bratislava multiple times, I can definitely say that the actual ranges from which a hospital can be accesed in 10 minutes are much smaller. This fact only highlights the importance of real life data collection and integration into such models.

The results of the second part of the project are two heatmaps comparing the perception of safety and hauntedness by gender. However, due to previously stated limitations, the comaprison by gender cannot be made from this map, as each gender covered a different area. Therefore, no actual finding has been made, as any difference between genders could be explained by difference in area. For future it is absolutely necessary to inform volunteers of exact purpose of the collected data to avoid similar mistakes. Another interesting observation was that even very detailed questions with multiple examples were understood differently by different people and therefore rated differently. This means that no matter what the data we collect are going to be subjective and the only thing we can do is to use them while taking our positionalities into account.

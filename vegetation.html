Vegetation Indices Mapping

This project maps the change in vegetation coverage and health of my home region in Slovakia between the years 2018 and 2025. I used Sentinel 2 multispectral satellite imagery to map the vegetation and calculate the Normalized Vegetation Difference Index (NDVI) to asses the health of vegetation. By subtracting the 2018 data from the data from 2025, I was able to map change in the NDVI and visualize which areas were deforested and where did the vegetation health increase.

Project Overview

This project uses multispectral satellite imagery from Sentinel 2 to monitor change in vegetation. Multispectral imagery collects reflected electromagneitc radiation from object on the ground. Different composition of objects cause them to reflect absorb and reflect different parts of electromagnetic spectra of sunlight, which acts as power source for such satellite imagery. By measuring different parts of spectra, which are in multispectral imagery called bands, composition of objects on the ground can be identified and visualized. This has widespread use in land use classification, agriculture planning, geology, coastal mapping, and urban development as changing which bands are visualized on the map changes what becomes highlighted. Specific use of multispectral imagery is in sustainable development, as it allows to assess the health of plants and vegetation. This is due to fact that healthier plants reflect more of the near infrared (NIR) spectrum and obsorb more of the visible red light. By changing the map colors from true color to false color composite showing (NIR) spectrum, we can see which plants are healthy without any on-site measurement. This allows for more efficient land use, better monitoring of nature conservation areas, and prevention of environmental crimes. These applications are simplified through use of vegetation indexes, which are mathematical formulas that transform multispectral data into informations about plant health, growth and density. I used Normalized Vegetation Difference Index (NDVI), which is the most common one in vegetation monitoring. NDVI is calculated by dividing the difference between red and NIR radiation by sum of those radiations and attains values between -1 an 1. Negative values represent non-vegetation surfaces and the health of the vegetation increases with higher NDVI values.

For this project I selected my home region in Slovakia and the tile coresponding to it. Tile is a area unit of Sentinel 2 satellites. Sentinel 2 is an European space mission consisting of two polar-orbiting satellites synchronised with the sun as they capture reflected sunlight. The satellites capture multispectral imagery of Earth surface in 290 km wide swaths covering the whole Earth surface in 5 days, with one satellite orbiting the Earth parallel to the equator and the other parallel to meridians. This coverage divides the Earth surface into squre shaped regions - tiles. To obtain the tiles, the data must be first processed through orthorectification software, which removes camera tilt, lens distortion, and topographical displacement and georeferences the images to correct locations. The result is an orthomosaic, which is a highly detailed 2D map aerial map. To create a usable orthomosaic from Sentinel 2 data, it is neccesary to create and average of the scans from certain time period. This is neccesary as often in individual scans there are some areas missing due to sensor malfunction or clouds. By averaging the data over one year, these errors can be eliminated and a good coverage of vegetation can be achieved, as the vegetation in different seasons absorbs and refletcs the light differently.

Methods

Satellite data were obtained and processed using Google Earth Engine using JavaScript code by Dr. Britta Ricker (available on GitHub). Resulting orthomosaics were uploaded to QGIS and NDVI was calculated using raster calculator tool. The NDVI change was calculated by subtracting 2018 NDVI from 2025 NDVI, resulting in positive values representing increase in vegetation coverage or health and negative values representing vegetation loss.

Results & Reflection

Overall, the NDVI index of the region increased, and in most areas it was an increase up to 0.3, which can be best explained by better plant health. New vegetation was likely planted in the dark green regions in the middle of the NDVI change map, while some parcels in the northwest were deforested. Interesting feature that becomes visible on the NDVI change map is the dark red line from center to southeastern corner of the map, which likely corespondes to a new road, which construction led to deforestation in that area. This change is not observable from the true color maps, but becomes highlighted in multispectral false color composite, being a good example of how multispectral imagery can be used to uncover environmental crimes.

I really liked working on this project, not only becuase it is connected to a region I am familiar with, but also because I see great potential use for the skills of working with multispectral satellite data. It is as if I suddenly gained the ability to see the world not only in the visible spectrum, but in all spectra from infrared to ultraviolet. I found fascinating the techique of assigning different spectral bands to red, green and blue colors and using it to highlight different features. I do not think much could be improved in this project.
